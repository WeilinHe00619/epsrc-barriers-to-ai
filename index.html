Exploring Barriers to

An interdisciplinary study by the University of Bristol uncovering the socio-economic, literacy, and structural factors shaping the future of AI research.

EPSRC | University of Bristol

Project Overview

The doctoral pipeline in Artificial Intelligence remains exclusive despite high demand. Our project examines why certain demographics are underrepresented in advanced AI studies, focusing on the transition from undergraduate to postgraduate research.

"Our goal is to inform future training provision and university policy to enhance participation and confidence in AI-related careers."

Core Dimensions

Socio-economic

Investigating how gender, race, and financial background form "invisible gates" that filter talent before the application stage.

AI Literacy

Utilising AILIT-S testing to assess self-efficacy and how perceived technical gaps act as psychological gatekeepers.

Structural Barriers

NLP analysis of 2,643 PhD listings to reveal systemic inequalities in recruitment, eligibility, and funding distribution.

Methodology

"Getting Started in AI" Training

Designed with the Jean Golding Institute to provide interventionist training for non-technical backgrounds.

Pre/Post Quantitative Tracking

Tracking shifts in confidence and study intentions through rigorous longitudinal surveys.

Qualitative Interviews

45-60 minute semi-structured interviews exploring career expectations and institutional policies.

Findings

01

Global Ambition vs. National Restriction

"While 79.2% of funded projects claim to be open globally, high visa costs and the 30% international recruitment cap create a 'False Hope' barrier."

02

The Structural Confidence Gap

"Even with identical academic backgrounds, students from lower socio-economic backgrounds show significantly lower self-efficacy in AI literacy self-assessments."
