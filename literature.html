Theoretical Framework

To understand why the doctoral pipeline remains exclusive despite high demand, this report examines barriers across three interconnected dimensions: Socio-Economic, Individual (AI Literacy), and Structural.

1. Socio-economic Barriers

Current literature highlights how systemic inequalities function as "invisible gates," filtering out talent long before the formal application stage. The intersection of gender, race, and financial background creates a compound disadvantage for prospective students.

1.1 The Gender Gap in AI

Research by Young et al. (2023) indicates a persistent gender gap in emergent AI professions. This disparity is not merely a pipeline issue but is reinforced by the cultural perception of AI as a male-dominated, high-stakes technical field. Women often report lower belongingness in computer science environments, which directly impacts their willingness to pursue doctoral studies.

1.2 Financial Capital & The Cost of Living

The "Cost of Living Crisis" has exacerbated barriers for students from lower socio-economic backgrounds.

  • Tuition Fees: High upfront costs for master's degrees (often a prerequisite for PhDs) act as a primary deterrent.
  • Unpaid Labour: The expectation to undertake unpaid internships to gain "relevant experience" privileges those who can afford to work for free.
  • Digital Divide: Access to high-performance computing hardware required for AI model training is often restricted to wealthy institutions or individuals.

2. Individual Barriers: AI Literacy

We conceptualise AI Literacy not just as technical competency (coding), but as a psychological gatekeeper. Using the AILIT-S framework (Hornberger et al., 2025), we distinguish between actual ability and perceived ability (Self-Efficacy).

"Even when students possess the necessary technical backgrounds, their perceived ability to succeed in AI often dictates their willingness to apply for doctoral programmes."

The "Black Box" Anxiety

Non-CS students often view AI as a "magic black box," leading to anxiety and self-exclusion. Our intervention posits that demystifying the underlying logic of algorithms (e.g., supervised vs. unsupervised learning) can significantly boost self-efficacy (Zhang et al., 2025).

3. Structural & Policy Barriers

Moving beyond individual choices, our primary analysis of 2,643 live PhD listings on FindAPhD.com reveals how institutional policies actively reinforce exclusion.

3.2 Visa Costs & Geography

Visa Costs: The rising Immigration Health Surcharge and student visa fees create a financial firewall, effectively barring international students from low-income backgrounds despite their academic merit.

Geographic Bias: Our NLP analysis also revealed a concentration of AI opportunities in the "Golden Triangle" (London, Oxford, Cambridge), limiting access for students in other UK regions and reinforcing regional inequalities.
